ROA and ROE

The ROA analyses profitability of the operations of a firm.

Shareholders however primarily care about how much they earn on their investment. Hence, profits are benchmarked against the company’s equity using return on equity (ROE):

Using profit before taxes yields the return on equity before taxes; using profit after taxes yields the return on equity after taxes.

A firm’s ROE can be higher or lower than its ROA. If a firm does not use debt to finance its assets, ROE and ROA are equal. In that situation, no interests are paid, so EBIT equals profits, and all assets are financed with equity.

Most firms do use some debt to finance a part of their assets, however. Then profits are lower than EBIT, but equity is also lower than assets. Whether this results in a higher or lower ROE compared to ROA depends on the effect of financial leverage:

The effect of the use of debt, or the financial leverage effect, is positive and leads to a higher ROE if the return on assets is higher than the cost of debt. The effect is stronger (either positively or negatively) when more debt is used.
