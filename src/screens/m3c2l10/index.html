FROM PRODUCTION COSTS TO COSTS OF GOODS SOLD

But what about the fourth, unsold sailboat? Aquasail still owns it, so it has to have some value. Remember that a profit-and-loss statement takes into account all expenses that match the revenues being reported.

At this point, there is a mismatch between the operating revenues, which relate to the sale of 3 boats, and the costs of production, which relate to the assembly of 4 boats. The difference is the 1 unsold boat.

In order to match revenues and expenses, we therefore have to subtract the cost of the 1 unsold boat from the total cost of production, to get the cost of the 3 boats that were sold. This allows to match revenues of 3 boats to the actual costs of these 3 boats. But what is the value of the unsold sailboat? Clearly, it’s not equal to its expected sales price, as Aquasail did not sell the boat yet. We only take into account the costs, not the sales margin.

The easiest way to calculate the cost of the sailboat is to add all direct inputs, to get its direct cost. Here, we add 1000 raw materials and 500 wages, and get a direct cost of 1500.

This cost is recorded in the profit-and-loss statement in the inventory changes finished goods line, below the cost of production.
