Profitability Level

We now have a way to calculate the profitability of a firm. But what is a satisfactory profitability level? We tackle this question in two different ways.

This can be easily explained. First, a shareholder requires at least the risk free interest rate, as reflected by e.g. government bonds. Obviously, investing in shares is not risk free as returns are not guaranteed. If the return for investing in risky shares would be lower than the return on government bonds, rational investors would switch to these riskless investments instead of investing in risky shares. Worst case, a shareholder may lose the total amount invested. Consequently, shareholders require a compensation for risk exposure by demanding a risk premium above the risk free rate.

Biotechnology start-ups or other new ventures usually have a very high expected ROE given their inherent risks: limited or no track record, unproven business model or management skills, uncertain revenue streams, lack of collateral,… Relatively mature companies in stable industries like consumer goods pose less risk and have thus a far lower cost of equity.

Note that the cost of equity is an opportunity cost related to the use of equity. It is not an out-of-pocket expense. While a firm may pay out dividends, the dividend alone will typically not be sufficient to cover the cost of equity. Shareholders expect an increase in the value of their shares – or capital gains - next to dividends. Dividends and capital gains are together the total return for shareholders.
