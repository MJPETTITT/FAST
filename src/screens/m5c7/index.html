Financial Leverage Effect

Let's illustrate the financial leverage effect with an example.

Scroll down to view all the information.

Company A

Company A has assets of 100 million EUR and is fully equity funded:

It is straightforward to calculate ROA, with
operating profit = €10 million

ROE is also 10%, as net profits equal operating profit, and equity equals assets. As there are no liabilities, the financial leverage effect is zero, i.e. ROE = ROA.

Company B

Now consider Company B, which is identical to A except that is has 50 million EUR of equity and a similar amount in bank debt with an interest rate of 5%. As Company B is identical to A, B’s operating profit also equals 10 million Euro.

Profit for the shareholders now amounts to 7.5 million EUR = 10 million EUR operating profit – 2.5 million interest paid (= 5% x 50 million EUR bank debt). While ROA is still 10%, ROE is now 15%.

For Company B, ROE exceeds ROA as the firm has financed half of its assets with debt that costs 5%, while it obtains an operating profit that is bigger than the cost of debt. Shareholders benefit from this excess return.

Summary

Putting it all together, ROE is determined by ROA and the financial leverage effect.

ROA, in turn, is determined by (1) margin and (2) asset turnover.

Finally, the financial leverage effect is determined by (1) the difference between ROA and the cost of liabilities, and (2) the proportion of liabilities to equity. It is clear that a good ROA is essential to achieve a high ROE!
