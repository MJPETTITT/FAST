A second approach to benchmarking profitability

A second approach is to benchmark the ROA with the firm’s average cost of financing, i.e. the weighted average cost of capital or WACC. Assets are typically financed with a mix of equity and debt. Both debtholders and shareholders seek a compensation for their investment in the firm.

The WACC represents the weighted average cost of all financial resources in a firm. It has two main components:

  • Cost of equity: the compensation (opportunity cost) required by shareholders.

  • Cost of debt: the interest rates charged by creditors.

To compute the WACC, we consider the relative importance of the various financing sources and their respective costs.

If ROA, for example 14%, exceeds the WACC, e.g. 13%, the firm creates value as operations enable to reward debtholders and shareholders. The excess return generated above the WACC allows to attribute more to shareholders than what they required, which will result in an increase in the share price and hence in capital gains for the shareholders.

On the contrary, if the WACC would be 15% with the same ROA of 14%, the firm is not in a position to fully compensate its financiers. As debtholders rank higher than shareholders, they get their full compensation.
