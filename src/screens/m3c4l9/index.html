FIXED ASSETS

Let’s look at the balance sheet in more detail. First, fixed assets, or all assets that have a lifetime that extends over more than a year, have decreased with the amount of depreciations. This reflects that the value of buildings and machinery decreases with their use.

Use the down arrow on the left to find out more.

CURRENT ASSETS

Second, current assets, which show all assets that are expected to remain in the company for less than 1 year, now comprise inventories, accounts receivable and cash.

Two types of inventories are shown: raw materials and finished goods, which amount to the direct cost of 5 unsold sailboats. Accounts receivable represent the invoice of 4400 sent to the customers who did not pay yet.

SHAREHOLDERS' EQUITY

Third, Aquasail generated 300 profits after taxes for its shareholders. A company can either decide to pay out the profits as dividends to its shareholders, or retain them in the company to put them to further use.

As a startup company, Aquasail decided not to pay any dividends yet. The profits are therefore shown as retained earnings and are part of the equity, which reflects that they eventually accrue to the shareholders.

LIABILITIES

Fourth, liabilities are composed of the unpaid invoice from suppliers, or the accounts payable, and the remaining bank debt amounting to 21000.

Remark that, for accounting purposes, this 21000 remaining bank debt is split in 2 parts: 1 part that is payable within the year, 3000, and the remainder that is due after a year, or 18000. As such, the balance sheet clearly distinguishes between all short term or current liabilities, and long term debt. Current liabilities show all short term liabilities, due within a year. Here, they are the sum of the bank debt that has to repaid to the bank within the year and the accounts payable.
