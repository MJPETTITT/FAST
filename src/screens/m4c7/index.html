OPTIMAL WORKING CAPITAL REQUIREMENT

One might wonder if there is an optimal level of working capital requirement. Well, it depends. The working capital requirement is namely contingent upon the industry in which a company operates as well as the company’s strategy and business model. Very often businesses need to make a trade-off between reducing the WCR or keep/increase it in order to pursue their strategies. Let's look at a couple of examples.

TOYOTA AND AUDI

It might be wise to implement JIT as in the Toyota example. However, then it might happen that not enough inventory is available to serve clients, potentially hurting the company’s reputation.

Another example: from a financial point of view it is advantageous to ask immediate cash payments from customers. However, this might destroy client relationships and hence reduce sales. Consequently, companies will need to find the optimal balance. Audi went a step further. It collects money from its customers for its WECARE insurance programme. It'll only have to pay its customers later, if anything happens.

PARMA HAM

Parma ham is considered to be one of the best hams in the world and subject to several quality criteria. One of those is that the ham needs to dry 12 months. This of course entails implications for its working capital requirement as shown in the figure. Parma ham is produced in the beginning of the operating cycle. Then, it remains for at least 12 months in ‘inventory’ after which it is sold to customers.

As the diagram shows, the cash used to buy the ham’s ingredients leaves the company long before the cash from sales flows in. Hence, the gap of at least 12 months needs to be bridged. The working capital requirement is thus huge and financing it is costly, and partly explains why the ham has a high sales price. Similar examples include the champagne and whisky industry.

CARREFOUR

French discounter Carrefour operates as a typical retailer: goods are purchased from suppliers and later sold to customers in its stores. While Carrefour, as a big market player, is able to obtain extensive supplier credits, its customers are ordinarily obliged to pay immediately. This yields an interesting situation in terms of working capital requirement: customers pay long before suppliers have to be paid.

SPONTANEOUS FINANCING

Instead of having a positive working capital requirement (WCR), Carrefour (like most retailers) actually has a negative requirement, i.e. it has a financing surplus with cash entering the company sooner than it is leaving. This means that Carrefour does not need to look for a positive net working capital, but instead it generates money via its operations.

DRIVEN BY OPERATIONS

These examples visibly clarify that working capital requirement is largely driven by a company’s operations. There is not really such a thing as the optimal WCR level, yet companies must optimise it in line with their business strategy.
