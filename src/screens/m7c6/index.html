Debt repayment capacity

In order to understand whether the level of debt is sustainable in the long term, we assess the debt repayment capacity. Whereas there are no hard payment commitments towards shareholders, firms need to honour all financial obligations. A firm’s ability to pay critically depends on its operating cash flow generation – the larger the cash flow, the more it can use to pay debt holders. Consequently, all ratios discussed below include operating cash flow in the numerator. We use operational cash flow and exclude other potential sources of cash like divestments or raising new funds as these do not represent a sustainable long term solution: you can only sell your assets once!

This ratio simply indicates which percentage of all outstanding liabilities can be paid for with the current amount of cash generated by the business.

However, some liabilities like accounts payable are operational in nature, implying that they are spontaneously renewed as long as the firm continues operations. Thus, it is advisable to focus on the financial debt a company owes as this is subject to a pre-set repayment schedule.

The ratio shows which debt percentage can be repaid every year or, conversely, how long it would take for the company to repay its financial debt, assuming a stable cash flow. A ratio of for example 40% implies that 40% of the financial debt can be repaid with the cash flow of a given year, or that it would take 2.5 years to reimburse all financial debt. This can then be compared with the average maturity of the outstanding debt.

In fact, only short term or current financial debt is maturing in the coming year. A safe financial situation requires that operational cash flow should thus at least cover short term financial debt. If not, the firm will have to look for additional financial resources or divest assets to meet its short term commitments. Additionally, no cash would be available for new investments or for paying dividends. This may be detrimental for a company as it will not be able to grow, let alone sustain its current assets level.
