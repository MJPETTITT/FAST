THE OPERATING CYCLE OF A COMPANY

How much NWC a firm should have, depends on its working capital requirement, which is driven by its operating cycle. This diagram shows the concept of working capital requirement graphically by providing an overview of the operating cycle of a company.

OPERATIONS

Two important dimensions are shown. The first is operations and refers to the actual production process. Raw materials are purchased at a specific date and are converted into finished goods, which are later sold to customers. During the period between the purchase date and selling date, the goods are in the inventory.

CASH

The second dimension is cash. This refers to the cash payments resulting from operations. Usually raw materials do not have to be paid to suppliers immediately. This is called supplier’s credit or 'payables' in the figure. The same concept holds for sales with companies granting customer’s credit, allowing customers to pay only after a certain period, shown as 'receivables' in the figure.

WORKING CAPITAL REQUIREMENT

As operations do not immediately lead to cash in- or outflows, there is a gap. The gap between cash out and cash in, caused by a company’s daily operations, is the working capital requirement.
